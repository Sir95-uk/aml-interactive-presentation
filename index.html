üèõÔ∏è AMLAR - Anti-Money Laundering Regulations ‚ñº

What is AMLAR?

AMLAR is the UK's comprehensive reform of Anti-Money Laundering regulations, replacing the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 (MLR 2017) with a modernized regulatory framework.

üéØ Key Changes Under AMLAR:

  • Supervision Reform: OPBAS will be abolished and replaced with a new FCA body that directly supervises professional enablers (accountants, lawyers, tax advisers)
  • Direct FCA Oversight: Professional body supervisors will no longer regulate AML compliance - FCA takes direct control
  • Crypto Asset Oversight: Comprehensive regulation of crypto asset businesses by FCA (since October 2023, with full conduct regulation coming 2024-2026)
  • Asset Recovery Powers: Enhanced powers for civil recovery and unexplained wealth orders
  • Risk-Based Approach: More flexible, proportionate requirements based on actual risk
  • Technology Integration: Recognition of digital identity verification and AI-powered monitoring

üîÑ MAJOR STRUCTURAL CHANGE - End of Professional Body Supervision:

Current System (Until AMLAR): Professional bodies (ICAEW, Law Society, etc.) supervise their members for AML, with OPBAS overseeing these bodies

New System (Under AMLAR): OPBAS abolished. FCA establishes new supervisory body to directly regulate professional enablers

Why this matters:

  • More consistent and rigorous supervision across the sector
  • Professional bodies can no longer "self-regulate" on AML
  • FCA will have direct enforcement powers over accountants, lawyers, and tax advisers
  • Stronger focus on high-risk professional enablers who facilitate money laundering
  • Better coordination with other FCA-supervised sectors

‚ö†Ô∏è Implementation Timeline:

2025-2026: Transition period for firms to adapt systems and processes

Key Action: Review and update your AML policies, procedures, and training materials to align with AMLAR requirements

‚úÖ What You Need to Do:

  • Conduct a gap analysis between current MLR 2017 compliance and AMLAR requirements
  • Update risk assessments to reflect new regulatory expectations
  • Review customer due diligence procedures
  • Ensure staff training covers AMLAR changes
  • Update monitoring and reporting systems
üåç FATF - Financial Action Task Force ‚ñº

What is FATF?

The Financial Action Task Force is the global standard-setter for anti-money laundering and counter-terrorist financing (AML/CFT). FATF issues recommendations that countries must implement to combat financial crime effectively.

üö® 2027 Mutual Evaluation Review (MER)

Critical Date: The UK will undergo its next FATF Mutual Evaluation Review in 2027

What will be assessed:

  • Effectiveness of AMLAR implementation
  • Quality and usefulness of Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs)
  • Crypto asset regulation and supervision
  • Beneficial ownership transparency
  • International cooperation and information sharing
  • Asset recovery and confiscation measures

‚ö†Ô∏è Why the 2027 MER Matters:

A poor rating could lead to:

  • International reputational damage
  • Increased scrutiny of UK financial institutions
  • Higher compliance costs for cross-border transactions
  • Potential "grey listing" if deficiencies are severe

üìã FATF 40 Recommendations - Key Areas:

  • R.1-2: Risk assessment and national cooperation
  • R.10-16: Customer due diligence and record keeping
  • R.20-23: Suspicious transaction reporting
  • R.24-25: Beneficial ownership transparency
  • R.26-35: Supervision and enforcement
üì¢ REP CRIM - Reporting Criminal Property ‚ñº

What is REP CRIM?

REP CRIM refers to the obligations under the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 (POCA) requiring authorized persons in the regulated sector to report suspicions of money laundering and criminal property.

üéØ Key Obligations:

  • Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs): Must be filed when you know or suspect money laundering
  • Authorization Required: Must obtain consent from NCA before proceeding with suspicious transactions
  • Tipping Off: Prohibited from disclosing that a SAR has been made
  • Record Keeping: Maintain records of all reports and decisions

‚öñÔ∏è Legal Consequences:

Failure to report: Up to 5 years imprisonment and/or unlimited fine

Tipping off: Up to 2 years imprisonment and/or unlimited fine

Prejudicing an investigation: Up to 5 years imprisonment and/or unlimited fine

‚ö†Ô∏è SAR Quality Initiative:

The NCA and law enforcement are focusing on improving SAR quality ahead of the 2027 FATF review. Ensure your SARs:

  • Contain sufficient detail to enable investigation
  • Explain the suspicion clearly
  • Include relevant supporting information
  • Are timely and accurate

‚úÖ Best Practices:

  • Appoint a nominated officer (MLRO) with appropriate authority
  • Provide regular training on spotting red flags
  • Maintain clear escalation procedures
  • Document all decisions, including reasons for not filing SARs
  • Review and test procedures regularly
üí∞ MSB - Money Service Businesses ‚ñº

What are MSBs?

Money Service Businesses include currency exchange, money transmission, and payment services providers. MSBs are primarily regulated by HMRC for AML purposes and must register with HMRC before operating.

üèõÔ∏è FCA's Role with MSBs:

Important: While HMRC is the primary AML supervisor for MSBs, the FCA has regulatory authority over certain MSB activities:

  • Payment Services: MSBs providing payment services (e.g., Payment Institutions) are regulated by the FCA under the Payment Services Regulations
  • E-Money: Electronic Money Institutions (EMIs) are authorized and supervised by the FCA
  • Crypto Assets: From October 2023, cryptoasset businesses operating in the UK are regulated by the FCA (see Crypto section below)
  • Dual Regulation: Some MSBs may be supervised by both HMRC (for AML) and FCA (for conduct/prudential)

‚ö†Ô∏è Understanding Your Regulator:

HMRC-supervised MSBs: Traditional money transmission, currency exchange (not requiring FCA authorization)

FCA-supervised MSBs: Payment institutions, e-money issuers, crypto asset firms

Action: Ensure you understand which regulator(s) you report to and comply with both AML and conduct requirements where applicable

üîÑ MSB Reform - Key Changes (2026):

  • Enhanced Registration: More rigorous fitness and propriety checks
  • Strengthened Supervision: Increased HMRC supervision and enforcement
  • Higher Standards: Stricter controls on high-risk MSBs
  • Technology Requirements: Enhanced transaction monitoring capabilities
  • International Standards: Alignment with FATF recommendations on MSBs

‚ö†Ô∏è High-Risk Areas for MSBs:

  • Remittance to high-risk jurisdictions
  • Cash-intensive operations
  • Virtual asset service providers (VASPs)
  • Lack of transparency in ownership
  • Inadequate transaction monitoring

üö® Enforcement Trends:

HMRC has significantly increased enforcement actions against non-compliant MSBs, including:

  • Registration cancellations
  • Civil penalties up to ¬£5 million
  • Criminal prosecutions for serious failures
  • Public warnings and naming of non-compliant businesses

‚úÖ Compliance Essentials for MSBs:

  • Register with HMRC and maintain valid registration
  • Implement robust customer due diligence
  • Maintain adequate transaction monitoring systems
  • Keep records for at least 5 years
  • File high-quality SARs when required
  • Conduct regular staff training
  • Maintain appropriate policies and procedures
‚Çø Crypto Assets & FCA Regulation ‚ñº

Crypto Asset Regulation in the UK

Since October 2023, cryptoasset businesses operating in the UK must be registered with the FCA and comply with AML regulations. This represents a major shift in how crypto is regulated.

üéØ What Changed in October 2023:

  • FCA Registration: All cryptoasset firms must register with the FCA (previously HMRC)
  • Cryptoasset Activities Covered: Exchange between crypto and fiat currency, crypto-to-crypto exchange, custody wallet services, participation in and provision of financial services related to cryptoassets
  • Enhanced Due Diligence: Stricter customer verification and transaction monitoring requirements
  • Travel Rule: Requirements to share customer information with other crypto service providers

‚ö†Ô∏è Future FCA Regulation - Expanding Beyond AML:

Currently: FCA regulates crypto firms for AML purposes only

Future (2024-2026): FCA will regulate crypto for conduct and prudential purposes under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000

What this means:

  • Crypto activities will be brought into the FCA's full regulatory perimeter
  • Consumer protection rules will apply
  • Financial promotions regime already in force (October 2023)
  • Prudential requirements (capital, liquidity) will apply
  • Conduct requirements similar to traditional financial services

üö® High-Risk Areas for Crypto:

FATF and FCA have identified these as particular concerns:

  • Anonymity-enhanced coins (AECs): Privacy coins and mixing services
  • Decentralized Finance (DeFi): Unclear AML obligations and lack of central control
  • Peer-to-peer transactions: Difficulty in identifying beneficial owners
  • Cross-border transfers: Challenges in applying travel rule requirements
  • Wallet custody: Self-hosted wallets and unhosted wallet transactions

‚úÖ Compliance Requirements for Crypto Firms:

  • FCA Registration: Must be registered before operating in UK
  • Customer Due Diligence: Enhanced verification for crypto customers
  • Transaction Monitoring: Systems to detect suspicious crypto transactions
  • Travel Rule Compliance: Share originator/beneficiary info for transfers over ¬£1,000
  • Sanctions Screening: Check wallets against OFSI and international sanctions lists
  • Record Keeping: Maintain records of all transactions for 5 years
  • SAR Filing: Report suspicious activity to the NCA
  • Staff Training: Ensure staff understand crypto-specific risks

üîÆ Looking Ahead - 2027 FATF Review:

The UK's approach to crypto regulation will be a major focus of the 2027 FATF Mutual Evaluation Review. FATF will assess:

  • Effectiveness of the FCA registration regime
  • Implementation of the travel rule
  • Supervision and enforcement actions
  • Ability to trace and seize crypto assets
  • International cooperation on crypto investigations

üèõÔ∏è OPBAS & The Future of Professional Enabler Supervision ‚ñº

üö® CRITICAL CHANGE: OPBAS Being Abolished

Under AMLAR, OPBAS will be abolished and replaced with direct FCA supervision of professional enablers.

This represents one of the most significant structural changes to UK AML supervision in decades.

What is OPBAS (Current System)?

OPBAS is the Office for Professional Body Anti-Money Laundering Supervision, established in January 2018 as part of the FCA. OPBAS oversees how 25+ professional body supervisors (PBS) regulate their members for AML compliance.

Current Structure: Professional bodies ‚Üí supervised by OPBAS ‚Üí overseen by FCA

üîÑ The New System Under AMLAR:

OPBAS Model Ends: Professional body supervision system abolished

New FCA Body Created: A new division within the FCA will directly supervise professional enablers

New Structure: Professional enablers ‚Üí directly supervised by FCA
